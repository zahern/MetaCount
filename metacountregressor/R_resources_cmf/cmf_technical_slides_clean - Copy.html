What Are Crash Modification Factors?

CMF Definition:

\[\text{CMF}(a \rightarrow b) = \frac{\hat{N}_b}{\hat{N}_a}\]

where:

  • \(a\) = the current condition a.
  • \(b\) = after a treatment/change
  • \(\hat{N}_a\) = predicted crashes in condition \(a\)
  • \(\hat{N}_b\) = predicted crashes in condition \(b\)

Why CMF changes:

  • If the treatment lowers predicted crashes (\(\hat{N}_b < \hat{N}_a\)), then CMF < 1.
  • If the treatment raises predicted crashes (\(\hat{N}_b > \hat{N}_a\)), then CMF > 1.

Quick Example: Coefficient To Percent

Interpretation

  • CMF = 1.0 → No change (neutral effect)
  • CMF < 1.0 → Fewer crashes (safer treatment)
  • CMF > 1.0 → More crashes (riskier treatment)

Percentage Change

\[\text{Percent Change} = 100 \times \left(\exp(\beta \cdot x) - 1\right)\]

Example:

  • Coefficient \(\beta = -0.08394\) for CURVES
  • For when \(x = 1\):
    • \(100 \times (\exp(-0.08394) - 1) = -8.05\%\) crashes

Conceptual Structure (Core Difference)

Traditional Model

\[ \log(\hat N_i) = \text{Single additive equation} \]

That is

\[\hat{N}_i = E_i \times \exp(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1i} + \beta_2 X_{2i} + \ldots)\]

Everything is estimated together:

  • Baseline crash propensity
  • Traffic exposure
  • Traffic elasticity
  • Geometry effects

Mechanisms are mixed.

Hierarchical CMF Model

\[ \hat N_i = \underbrace{\exp(\text{baseline features})}_{\textbf{Inherent Risk}} \times \underbrace{AADT^{(\text{elasticity modified by geometry})}}_{\textbf{Traffic Response}} \]

Two distinct mechanisms:

  • Baseline block → inherent crash propensity
  • Traffic-response block → how crashes scale with traffic

This structural separation is the advantage. As we can explicitly model In response to AADT

Traditional model typically models with respect to Exposure of ADDT

What the Exposure (Offset) Really Does

Traditional model with exposure:

\[ \hat N_i = E_i \times \exp(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1i} + \dots) \]

If we assume AADT by segmentation length to be the exposure:

\[ E_i = AADT_i \times LENGTH_i \]

Then:

\[ \log(\hat N_i) = \log(AADT_i) + \log(LENGTH_i) + \text{linear predictor} \]

Important

Including AADT in the offset:

  • Fixes traffic elasticity at 1
  • Assumes proportional exposure
  • Prevents geometry from modifying traffic scaling directly as its mixed in with other exposure effects

It assumes:

If traffic doubles, crashes double — regardless of geometry.

If We Remove AADT from the Offset

Without offset:

\[ \log(\hat N_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1i} + \beta_{AADT} \log(AADT_i) \]

Now traffic elasticity is estimated.

But:

  • Still one pooled coefficient
  • Geometry does not structurally modify elasticity
  • Baseline and traffic effects remain mixed

Traditional can estimate elasticity, but only relative to its indivodual components. It cannot separate mechanisms. Ie geometric effects change crash likilohood neglicting traffic

What the Hierarchical CMF Structure Allows

Baseline block:

\[ \hat N_i^{\text{baseline}} = \exp(\alpha_0 + \alpha_1 X_{1i} + \dots) \]

Traffic-response block:

\[ \hat N_i = \hat N_i^{\text{baseline}} \times AADT_i^{(\gamma_0 + \gamma_1 \text{WIDTH}_i + \gamma_2 \text{CURVES}_i)} \]

Now geometry can:

  • Affect inherent crash risk through the baseline
  • Independently modify traffic elasticity

Traffic scaling becomes geometry-dependent. Eg,showes how changes in width and cuves adjust the elasticity directly in response to AADT

The Problem: Traditional Models

Traditional negative binomial regression:

\[\hat{N}_i = E_i \times \exp(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1i} + \beta_2 X_{2i} + \ldots)\]

Three Critical Questions

  1. What causes the coefficient?
    • Does CURVES increase crashes directly?
    • Or does it reflect traffic volume confounding (AADT)?
  2. How do we design interventions?
    • Should we prioritize geometry changes or traffic management?
    • Are both quantified?
  3. What if exposure assumptions are wrong?
    • Exposure effect should account for differences in road segmentation
    • But maybe longer roads have different safety dynamics

Solution: Hierarchical CMF Model

Two-Level Structure

Level 1 — Baseline Block (inherent crash propensity): \[\hat{N}_i^{\text{baseline}} = \exp(\alpha_0 + \alpha_{\text{URB}} \cdot \text{URB}_i + \alpha_{\text{ACCESS}} \cdot \text{ACCESS}_i + \cdots)\]

Level 2 — Traffic-Response Block (AADT elasticity): \[\hat{N}_i = \hat{N}_i^{\text{baseline}} \times \text{AADT}_i^{(\gamma_0 + \gamma_{\text{WIDTH}} \cdot \text{WIDTH}_i + \cdots)}\]

Key Advantage

  • Baseline features (URB, ACCESS, …) → What geometric factors directly explain the crash process
  • AADT features (WIDTH, CURVES, …) → How does the geometric factors affect the traffic volume sensitivity change?

Variable Meanings

  • URB: Urban indicator (1 = urban segment, 0 = non-urban segment).
  • ACCESS: Access-point level/density on the segment (more driveways/intersections).
  • GRADEBR: Grade-break level on the segment (more vertical grade interruptions).
  • CURVES: Horizontal curve intensity/count on the segment.
  • WIDTH: Lane/roadway width measure.
  • AADT: Annual Average Daily Traffic (vehicles/day).
  • LENGTH: Segment length exposure used to normalize crash counts.
  • OFFSET: log(AADT*LENGTH)

Where WIDTH Appears

WIDTH enters two mechanisms.

Baseline Effect

\[ \exp(-1.947 \cdot \text{WIDTH}) \]

Interpretation:

Wider lanes reduce inherent crash propensity.

Traffic Elasticity Effect

\[ AADT^{(0.175 \cdot \text{WIDTH})} \]

Interpretation:

Wider lanes increase elasticity of crashes with respect to traffic.

Full WIDTH Effect

\[ \exp(-1.947 \cdot \text{WIDTH}) \times AADT^{(0.175 \cdot \text{WIDTH})} \]

The net effect depends on traffic level.

Model Characteristics (CMF)

  1. Elasticity estimated, not fixed
  2. Geometry modifies traffic scaling
  3. Baseline and exposure effects separated
  4. Treatment effects vary by AADT
  5. Exposure proportionality becomes testable

Crash generation is decomposed structurally.

Example: Curve Density — Why the Story Changes

Traditional model (with offset):

CURVES coefficient:

\[ \beta = +0.0078 \]

\[ 100(\exp(0.0078)-1)=+0.78\% \]

Interpretation:

More curves increase crashes.

CMF hierarchical model:

CURVES modifies AADT elasticity:

\[ AADT^{(-0.0083 \cdot CURVES)} \]

At median AADT (23,771):

\[ 23771^{-0.0083} = 0.9205 \]

\[ \Rightarrow -8.05\% \text{ crashes} \]

Interpretation:

Curves reduce traffic sensitivity.

What Is Gained?

Not better percentages.

Not dramatic BIC improvement.

What is gained:

  • Separation of baseline vs traffic mechanisms
  • Traffic-dependent CMFs
  • Transparent exposure testing
  • Policy-level prioritization capability

Crash risk can be written as:

\[ \text{Crash Risk} = \textbf{Inherent Roadway Propensity} \times \textbf{Traffic Scaling Behavior} \]

Traditional NB estimates this jointly.

Hierarchical CMF estimates each component separately.
