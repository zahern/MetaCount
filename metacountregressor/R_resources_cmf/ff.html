Hierarchical Crash Modification Factors

Your Name

Motivation

Crash models typically estimate:

  • Geometry effects
  • Traffic effects
  • Exposure effects

But they do not separate:

  • Inherent crash propensity
  • Traffic scaling behavior

This presentation shows why that separation matters.

Crash Modification Factors (CMF)

Definition:

\[ \text{CMF}(a \rightarrow b) = \frac{\hat{N}_b}{\hat{N}_a} \]

Percent change:

\[ \%\text{ change} = 100 \times \left(\exp(\beta x) - 1\right) \]

Both traditional and hierarchical models can compute CMFs.

The difference is structural interpretation.

Conceptual Structure

Traditional Model

\[ \log(\hat N_i) = \text{Single additive equation} \]

Baseline risk, traffic exposure, and geometry are mixed together.

Hierarchical CMF Model

\[ \hat N_i = \underbrace{\exp(\text{baseline features})}_{\textbf{Inherent Risk}} \times \underbrace{AADT_i^{(\text{elasticity modified by geometry})}}_{\textbf{Traffic Response}} \]

Two mechanisms are modeled separately.

Traditional Model with Offset

Model form:

\[ \log(\hat N_i) = \log(AADT_i) + \log(LENGTH_i) + \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{WIDTH}_i + \beta_2 \text{CURVES}_i + \beta_3 \text{SHOULDER}_i \]

Key Property

Including AADT in the offset:

  • Fixes traffic elasticity at 1
  • Assumes proportional exposure
  • Geometry cannot modify traffic scaling

If traffic doubles, crashes double.

Fitted Traditional Model

Estimated Coefficients

\[ \beta_0 = -3.214 \]

\[ \beta_{\text{WIDTH}} = -0.065 \]

\[ \beta_{\text{CURVES}} = +0.0078 \]

\[ \beta_{\text{SHOULDER}} = -0.041 \]

Model Characteristics

  • Elasticity fixed at 1
  • Constant percent effects
  • No traffic-dependent treatment variation

Hierarchical CMF Model (Full Equation)

\[ \hat N_i = \exp( \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \text{WIDTH}_i + \alpha_2 \text{CURVES}_i + \alpha_3 \text{SHOULDER}_i ) \times AADT_i^{ ( \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 \text{WIDTH}_i + \gamma_2 \text{CURVES}_i ) } \times LENGTH_i \]

Baseline and traffic-response are separated.

Estimated CMF Coefficients

Baseline Block

\[ \alpha_0 = -5.102 \]

\[ \alpha_{\text{WIDTH}} = -1.947 \]

\[ \alpha_{\text{CURVES}} = +0.312 \]

\[ \alpha_{\text{SHOULDER}} = -0.088 \]

Traffic-Response Block

\[ \gamma_0 = 0.981 \]

\[ \gamma_{\text{WIDTH}} = +0.175 \]

\[ \gamma_{\text{CURVES}} = -0.0083 \]

Elasticity is estimated and geometry-dependent.

Where WIDTH Appears

WIDTH enters two mechanisms.

Baseline Effect

\[ \exp(-1.947 \cdot \text{WIDTH}) \]

Interpretation:

Wider lanes reduce inherent crash propensity.

Traffic Elasticity Effect

\[ AADT^{(0.175 \cdot \text{WIDTH})} \]

Interpretation:

Wider lanes increase elasticity of crashes with respect to traffic.

Full WIDTH Effect

\[ \exp(-1.947 \cdot \text{WIDTH}) \times AADT^{(0.175 \cdot \text{WIDTH})} \]

The net effect depends on traffic level.

Model Characteristics (CMF)

  1. Elasticity estimated, not fixed
  2. Geometry modifies traffic scaling
  3. Baseline and exposure effects separated
  4. Treatment effects vary by AADT
  5. Exposure proportionality becomes testable

Crash generation is decomposed structurally.

Example: Lane Widening (+1 SD)

Traditional model:

\[ \exp(-0.065) = 0.937 \]

\[ \Rightarrow -6.3\% \text{ at all traffic levels} \]

CMF Model — Lane Widening

Total effect:

\[ \exp(-1.947) \times AADT^{0.175} \]

At Median AADT (23,771)

\[ \exp(-1.947) \times 23771^{0.175} = 0.832 \]

\[ \Rightarrow -16.8\% \]

At High AADT (60,000)

\[ \exp(-1.947) \times 60000^{0.175} \approx 0.78 \]

\[ \Rightarrow -22\% \]

Why This Matters

Traditional model:

  • Same -6.3% everywhere

CMF model:

  • -16.8% at median traffic
  • -22% at high traffic

Now treatment prioritization can depend on traffic level.

Traditional model cannot vary treatment magnitude by AADT.

Example: Curves

Traditional:

\[ \exp(0.0078) = 1.0078 \]

\[ \Rightarrow +0.78\% \]

Interpretation:

Curves increase crashes.

CMF Interpretation of Curves

Traffic-response term:

\[ AADT^{-0.0083 \cdot \text{CURVES}} \]

At median AADT:

\[ 23771^{-0.0083} = 0.9205 \]

\[ \Rightarrow -8.05\% \]

Curves reduce traffic sensitivity.

Traditional model cannot express this mechanism.

Final Takeaway

Crash risk can be written as:

\[ \text{Crash Risk} = \textbf{Inherent Roadway Propensity} \times \textbf{Traffic Scaling Behavior} \]

Traditional negative binomial models estimate this jointly.

Hierarchical CMF models estimate each component separately.

That separation is the advantage.
