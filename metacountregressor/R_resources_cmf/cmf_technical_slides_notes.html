What Are Crash Modification Factors?

CMF Definition:

\[\text{CMF}(a \rightarrow b) = \frac{\hat{N}_b}{\hat{N}_a}\]

where:

  • \(a\) = the “before” condition (current design)
  • \(b\) = the “after” condition (after a treatment/change)
  • \(\hat{N}_a\) = predicted crashes in condition \(a\)
  • \(\hat{N}_b\) = predicted crashes in condition \(b\)

Why CMF changes:

  • If the treatment lowers predicted crashes (\(\hat{N}_b < \hat{N}_a\)), then CMF < 1.
  • If the treatment raises predicted crashes (\(\hat{N}_b > \hat{N}_a\)), then CMF > 1.

Quick example:

  • Before: \(\hat{N}_a = 100\) crashes/year
  • After: \(\hat{N}_b = 92\) crashes/year
  • CMF \(= 92/100 = 0.92 \Rightarrow -8\%\) crashes

Speaker note: - Define a as before and b as after. - CMF is a ratio of predicted crashes after vs before. - CMF < 1 means predicted crash reduction; CMF > 1 means increase.

Quick Example: Coefficient To Percent (Step By Step)

Interpretation

  • CMF = 1.0 → No change (neutral effect)
  • CMF < 1.0 → Fewer crashes (safer treatment) ✅
  • CMF > 1.0 → More crashes (riskier treatment) ❌

Percentage Change

\[\text{Percent Change} = 100 \times \left(\exp(\beta \cdot x) - 1\right)\]

Example: Coefficient \(\beta = -0.08394\) for CURVES - For \(x = 1\): \(100 \times (\exp(-0.08394) - 1) = -8.05\%\) crashes

The Problem: Traditional Models

Traditional negative binomial regression:

\[\hat{N}_i = E_i \times \exp(\beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{1i} + \beta_2 X_{2i} + \ldots)\]

Three Critical Questions

  1. What causes the coefficient?
    • Does CURVES increase crashes directly?
    • Or does it reflect traffic volume confounding?
  2. How do we design interventions?
    • Should we prioritize geometry changes or traffic management?
    • Are both quantified?
  3. What if exposure (length) assumptions are wrong?
    • Offset term treats all segments equally
    • But maybe longer roads have different safety dynamics

Result: Ambiguous, hard-to-communicate CMFs

Solution: Hierarchical CMF Model

Two-Level Structure

Level 1 — Baseline Block (inherent crash propensity): \[\hat{N}_i^{\text{baseline}} = \exp(\alpha_0 + \alpha_{\text{URB}} \cdot \text{URB}_i + \alpha_{\text{ACCESS}} \cdot \text{ACCESS}_i + \cdots)\]

Level 2 — Traffic-Response Block (AADT elasticity): \[\hat{N}_i = \hat{N}_i^{\text{baseline}} \times \text{AADT}_i^{(\gamma_0 + \gamma_{\text{WIDTH}} \cdot \text{WIDTH}_i + \cdots)}\]

Key Advantage

  • Baseline features (URB, ACCESS, GRADEBR) → What makes a road inherently risky?
  • Response features (WIDTH, CURVES) → How does traffic volume sensitivity change?

Variable Meanings (Plain English)

  • URB: Urban indicator (1 = urban segment, 0 = non-urban segment).
  • ACCESS: Access-point level/density on the segment (more driveways/intersections).
  • GRADEBR: Grade-break level on the segment (more vertical grade interruptions).
  • CURVES: Horizontal curve intensity/count on the segment.
  • WIDTH: Lane/roadway width measure.
  • AADT: Annual Average Daily Traffic (vehicles/day).
  • LENGTH / OFFSET: Segment length exposure used to normalize crash counts.

Audience cue: coefficients tell you how predicted crashes change when one variable increases by one unit, holding others fixed.

Speaker note: - URB is a context indicator, not a treatment. - ACCESS and GRADEBR are intensity measures of roadway conditions. - CURVES and WIDTH are geometry levers that audience can picture directly.

Why Offset Matters For AADT (Very Important)

In your original dataset, the offset is not just length.

Empirically from the data:

\[ \mathrm{OFFSET} = \log(AADT \times LENGTH) - 12.5208 \]

So the traditional model with offset is effectively:

\[ \hat N_i = \exp(\text{OFFSET}_i)\times\exp(\text{linear predictor}) \]

Meaning part of AADT exposure is already embedded before coefficient estimation.

What changes when you remove offset:

  1. AADT exposure is no longer pre-fixed by the offset term.
  2. CMF response block can estimate AADT sensitivity directly from data.
  3. That is why offset/no-offset comparisons can materially change CURVES/WIDTH effects.

Speaker note: - This is the key structural point of the talk. - With offset, part of AADT effect is pre-embedded. - Without offset, AADT effect must be explained by fitted model terms.

What’s Happening?

  • In CMF, each variable can have baseline and AADT-response components.
  • So the net effect is traffic-level dependent, not one pooled coefficient.
  • That is the core interpretability gain in a harmonized comparison.

Exactly Where Do The Numbers Come From?

Traditional model Hierarchical CMF model

  1. Fit step (model output): estimate coefficients \(\beta\) from data.
  • Traditional CURVES: \(\beta\approx+0.056\)
  • CMF CURVES: baseline \(-0.993\) and local \(+0.123\)
  1. Convert coefficient to CMF:

\[ \mathrm{CMF}_{(+1)} = \exp(\beta) \]

  1. Convert CMF to percent change:

\[ \%\text{ change} = 100\times(\text{CMF}-1)=100\times(\exp(\beta)-1) \]

  1. Substitute fitted values (rounded):
  • Traditional: \(100(\exp(0.056)-1)=+5.8\%\)
  • CMF (median AADT): \(100(\exp(-0.993+0.123\log(23771))-1)=+28.2\%\)

For AADT-response terms (for example WIDTH local term), use:

\[ \%\text{ change} = 100\times\left(\exp\left(\beta_{local}\log(AADT)\right)-1\right) \]

At \(AADT=23{,}771\), WIDTH net effect is:

\[ 100\times\left(\exp\left(-1.947 + 0.175\log(23771)\right)-1\right)= -16.8\% \]

Speaker note: - Read this as a recipe: fit beta -> exponentiate -> convert to percent. - For CURVES, compare +5.8% vs +28.2% to show traffic-dependent CMF response.

Both Models Give Percent Change. So What Is Gained?

Traditional model Hierarchical CMF model

Yes, both models can report:

\[ \%\text{ change} = 100\times\left(\exp(\beta\,x)-1\right) \]

The gain from CMF is not “having a percent.”
The gain is what that percent means mechanistically:

  1. Traditional NB: one pooled coefficient; baseline risk and traffic scaling are mixed.
  2. Hierarchical CMF: two blocks; baseline risk is separated from AADT response.
  3. Decision value: you can say whether a treatment changes:
  • inherent crash propensity, or
  • traffic-volume sensitivity.

That is why CMF percentages are more actionable for design policy.

Benefit 2: Better Intervention Planning

Scenario: Road Improvement ($500K budget)

Option A: Reduce CURVES by 1 unit on the common scale (curve mitigation)

Option B: Increase WIDTH by 1 unit on the common scale (lane widening)

Traditional Model Guidance

  • CURVES: \(\beta=+0.056\) → -1 gives \(\exp(0.056 \times -1)=0.946\) → -5.4%
  • WIDTH: \(\beta=-0.065\) → +1 gives \(\exp(-0.065 \times 1)=0.937\) → -6.3%
  • Decision: Both options are quantified; choose by cost/feasibility if effects are close.

CMF Hierarchical Guidance

  • CURVES: net effect depends on AADT through baseline + local terms.
    • At median AADT (23,771): +1 CURVES ≈ +28.2% crashes, so -1 CURVES ≈ -22.0%.
  • WIDTH: also traffic-dependent through baseline + local terms.
    • At median AADT (23,771): +1 WIDTH ≈ -16.8% crashes.
  • Decision: Both levers are quantified; CMF adds traffic-aware prioritization across corridors.
